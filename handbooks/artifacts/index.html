Chapter I

The Nature of Magic Items

Magic items are the lifeblood of adventure. From the lowliest +1 dagger pulled from a goblin's hoard to the world-shattering artifacts that have toppled empires and slain gods, these objects represent the convergence of arcane power and physical form. This compendium serves as your definitive guide to understanding, identifying, crafting, and wielding the magical arms and armor of the realms.

Every magic item carries within it a spark of the Weave — that invisible lattice of magical energy that permeates all of existence. When a skilled artificer, wizard, or divine agent binds that energy into an object, the result transcends the mundane. Steel becomes unbreakable. Leather turns aside dragon's breath. A simple wooden staff channels enough arcane force to level a castle.

"I have held in my hands a blade so cold it froze the tears on my cheeks, and a ring so warm it thawed the ice in my heart. The tools of the arcane are not mere objects — they are companions, burdens, and sometimes jailers."
— Fousio Faranthis, Acquisitor of Baldur's Gate

Artifacts vs. Legendary Items

Artifacts occupy a tier beyond legendary. They are unique objects — often only one exists in all the multiverse. An artifact cannot be destroyed by ordinary means. Each has a specific, often convoluted method of destruction that may itself require an entire quest to accomplish. Artifacts also carry random beneficial and detrimental properties that make each one unpredictable and dangerous.

Attunement

Some magic items require a creature to form a bond with them before their magical properties can be used. This bond is called attunement. Without it, a creature gains only the item's nonmagical benefits.

Rules of Attunement

Process. Attuning to an item requires a creature to spend a short rest focused on only that item while being in physical contact with it. This focus can take the form of weapon practice, meditation, prayer, or similar activity appropriate to the item.

Limit. A creature can be attuned to no more than three magic items at a time. Any attempt to attune to a fourth item fails — the creature must end its attunement to an existing item first.

Exclusivity. An item can be attuned to only one creature at a time. A creature can't attune to more than one copy of the same item.

Prerequisites. Some items require the wielder to belong to a specific class, possess a certain alignment, or meet other conditions before attunement can occur.

Ending Attunement. Attunement ends if the creature no longer satisfies the prerequisites, if another creature attunes to the item, if the creature is more than 100 feet from the item for 24 hours, if the creature dies, or if the creature voluntarily ends the bond during a short rest.

Identifying Magic Items

The most common method of identifying a magic item is the identify spell. However, a short rest spent focusing on an item also reveals its properties and how to use them, along with the number of charges it holds (if any). Potions are an exception — a small taste is enough to identify one.

Some items bear visual cues to their magical nature: faintly glowing runes, an unusual heft, warmth or cold to the touch, or an aura visible under detect magic. A cursed item, however, typically appears beneficial until its true nature manifests.

Variant: More Difficult Identification

Under this variant rule, a short rest does not reveal an item's properties. The identify spell, an Arcana check (DC set by the DM based on rarity), or experimentation over time are the only methods. This variant makes items more mysterious and can enhance the drama of discovering a powerful artifact.

Chapter II

Weapons of Legend

The weapons in this chapter represent the pinnacle of magical armament — legendary instruments of war that have turned the tides of history. Each entry includes a full mechanical stat block, lore, and design notes for Dungeon Masters seeking to introduce these items into their campaigns.

Vorpal Sword

Vorpal Sword Legendary
Weapon (any sword that deals slashing damage), legendary (requires attunement)

You gain a +3 bonus to attack and damage rolls made with this magic weapon. In addition, the weapon ignores resistance to slashing damage.

Vorpal Strike. When you attack a creature that has at least one head with this weapon and roll a 20 on the attack roll, you cut off one of the creature's heads. The creature dies if it can't survive without the lost head. A creature is immune to this effect if it is immune to slashing damage, doesn't have or need a head, has legendary actions, or the DM decides that the creature is too big for its head to be cut off with this weapon. Such a creature instead takes an extra 6d8 slashing damage from the hit.

Type: Slashing Sword
Attunement: Yes
Weight: Varies by base weapon
Offense
Defense

The vorpal sword is the quintessential killing weapon. Its ability to decapitate on a natural 20 makes it one of the most feared weapons in existence. Against creatures without legendary actions, a single lucky strike can end an encounter outright — no saving throw, no death saves, just a severed head rolling across the floor. Against sturdier foes with legendary actions, the 6d8 bonus damage on a critical is still devastating.

Blackrazor

Blackrazor Legendary
Weapon (greatsword), legendary (requires attunement by a creature of non-lawful alignment)

You gain a +3 bonus to attack and damage rolls made with this magic weapon. It has the following additional properties.

A creature whose soul has been devoured can be restored only by a wish spell. When it devours a soul, you gain temporary hit points equal to the slain creature's hit point maximum. Soul Awareness. While you hold Blackrazor, you are aware of the presence of Tiny or larger creatures within 60 feet that aren't constructs or undead.

Holy Avenger

Holy Avenger Legendary
Weapon (any sword), legendary (requires attunement by a paladin)

You gain a +3 bonus to attack and damage rolls made with this magic weapon. When you hit a fiend or an undead with it, that creature takes an extra 2d10 radiant damage.

Protective Aura. While you hold the drawn sword, it creates a 10-foot-radius aura around you. Staff of the Magi

Staff of the Magi Legendary
Staff, legendary (requires attunement by a sorcerer, warlock, or wizard)

This staff can be wielded as a magic quarterstaff that grants a +2 bonus to attack and damage rolls made with it. While you hold it, you gain a +2 bonus to spell attack rolls.

Spell Absorption. While holding the staff, you have advantage on saving throws against spells. In addition, you can use your reaction when another creature casts a spell that targets only you. If you do, the staff absorbs the magic of the spell, canceling its effect and gaining a number of charges equal to the absorbed spell's level. If this brings the staff's total charges above 50, the staff explodes (see Retributive Strike).

Charges. The staff has 50 charges for the following properties. It regains 4d6 + 2 expended charges daily at dawn. If you expend the last charge, roll a d20. On a 20, the staff regains 1d12 + 1 charges.

At Will (0 charges): arcane lock, detect magic, enlarge/reduce, light, mage hand, protection from evil and good
1 charge each: dispel magic, fireball (7th level), flaming sphere, ice storm, invisibility, knock, lightning bolt (7th level), passwall, web
4 charges: conjure elemental, telekinesis
7 charges: plane shift

Retributive Strike. You can break the staff over your knee as an action, triggering a devastating explosion. Each creature in a 30-foot-radius sphere centered on you takes force damage equal to 16 × the remaining charges. You have a 50% chance of being transported to a random plane instead of taking damage; otherwise you take the full damage.

Max Charges: 50
Recharge: 4d6+2 at dawn
Spell Save DC: Uses your own
The Retributive Strike: Last Resort

Breaking the Staff of the Magi is perhaps the single most devastating action a player character can take. With all 50 charges, the explosion deals 800 force damage within a 30-foot sphere. Even divided (you take half, split among all creatures), this is enough to obliterate virtually anything. The 50% chance of planar travel instead of death makes it a legendary gamble — the stuff campaigns are made of.

Chapter III

Artifacts of the Ages

Artifacts transcend legendary status. Each is unique — forged by gods, shaped by cataclysmic events, or imbued with the essence of beings whose power defies mortal comprehension. An artifact cannot be destroyed by any ordinary means. Each has specific destruction conditions that often require quests unto themselves.

All artifacts carry random properties — beneficial and detrimental — rolled or selected by the DM. These properties are in addition to the artifact's fixed abilities and make each one unique even across different campaigns.

Sword of Kas

Sword of Kas Artifact
Weapon (longsword), artifact (requires attunement)

You gain a +3 bonus to attack and damage rolls made with this magic weapon, which deals an extra 2d10 slashing damage to undead.

Random Properties: 1 minor beneficial, 1 major beneficial, 1 minor detrimental, 1 major detrimental.

Spirit of Kas. While attuned, your Strength score becomes 22 (if not already higher). You gain advantage on attack rolls against Vecna and advantage on saving throws against spells cast by Vecna.

Spells. While the sword is on your person, you can cast: call lightning, divine word, and finger of death. Once used, a property can't be used again until the next dawn.

Sentient: Yes (Chaotic Evil)
INT: 15
WIS: 13
CHA: 16
Purpose: Destroy Vecna

Personality. The sword hungers for revenge against Vecna above all else. It will attempt to dominate any wielder who does not pursue the destruction of the archlich. It communicates telepathically and can attempt to charm its wielder during moments of weakness.

Destroying the Sword. A creature attuned to both the Eye and Hand of Vecna can use the wish property of those combined artifacts to unmake the Sword of Kas. This is the only known method of destroying it.

Eye & Hand of Vecna

Eye of Vecna Artifact
Wondrous item, artifact (requires attunement)

Attaching the Eye. To attune to the eye, you must gouge out your own eye and press the artifact into your empty socket. The eye grafts itself to your skull and remains until you die.

Properties. While attuned, your alignment shifts one step toward neutral evil. You gain truesight out to 120 feet. You can use an action to see as if wearing a ring of X-ray vision. You can cast clairvoyance at will.

Random Properties: 1 minor beneficial, 1 major beneficial, 1 minor detrimental.

Hand of Vecna Artifact
Wondrous item, artifact (requires attunement)

Attaching the Hand. To attune to the hand, you must lop off your left hand at the wrist and press the artifact against the stump. The hand grafts itself to your arm and becomes fully functional.

Properties. While attuned, your Strength becomes 20 (if not already higher). Any melee spell attack you make with the hand deals an extra 2d8 cold damage. You can cast finger of death and sleep (as a 5th-level spell) at will.

Combined Power. If a creature is attuned to both the Hand and Eye simultaneously, it can cast wish once every 30 days. Using it for any purpose other than duplicating a spell has a 5% chance of causing Vecna to appear and reclaim both artifacts.

Orb of Dragonkind

Orb of Dragonkind Artifact
Wondrous item, artifact (requires attunement)

Random Properties: 2 minor beneficial, 1 minor detrimental, 1 major detrimental.

Spells. The orb has 7 charges and regains 1d4 + 3 charges daily at dawn. While attuned, you can expend 1+ charges to cast: cure wounds (5th level, 3 charges), daylight (1 charge), death ward (2 charges), scrying (3 charges).

Call Dragons. While controlling the orb, you can use an action to call all dragons within 40 miles. Each dragon must make a DC 18 Wisdom saving throw or be compelled to travel toward you by the most expedient means.

Control. You must succeed on a DC 15 Charisma check when first attuning. On failure, you become charmed by the orb (DC 18 suggestion at will on you).

Destroying the Orb. An orb can be destroyed only by crushing it beneath the foot of a dragon of the same type as the dragon essence trapped within it, and that dragon must willingly do so.

Axe of the Dwarvish Lords

Axe of the Dwarvish Lords Artifact
Weapon (battleaxe), artifact (requires attunement by a dwarf)

You gain a +3 bonus to attack and damage rolls made with this magic axe. The axe functions as a dwarven thrower and a sword of sharpness (adapted for an axe).

Random Properties: 2 minor beneficial, 1 major beneficial, 2 minor detrimental.

Conjure Earth Elemental. If you are holding the axe, you can cast conjure elemental (earth elemental only) once per dawn.

Travel the Depths. You can cast teleport once per 3 days. The destination must be within a mountain, hill, or underground structure you have visited.

Curse. Non-dwarf creatures that attune to the axe experience gradual physical transformation: facial hair growth, height reduction by 1 inch per day (to a minimum of 4 feet), and a compulsion to braid their hair. These effects reverse only if the axe's attunement ends.

Destroying the Axe. The only way to destroy the axe is to melt it in the magma forge of the dwarven god Moradin — an act that Moradin must personally sanction.

The Deck of Many Things: Handle with Care

The Deck of Many Things is perhaps the most campaign-altering item in the entire game. A single draw can grant a keep, a rare magic item, or the service of a warrior — or it can imprison your soul, strip you of all possessions, or pit you against an avatar of death. Many DMs approach this item with extreme caution, and for good reason: it can upend a campaign in a single moment. Use it as a narrative climax, not a random treasure drop.

Chapter V

Sentient Items

Some magic items possess sentience and personality — they think, they feel, they scheme. Such an item might be possessed, haunted by the spirit of a previous owner, or rendered self-aware through the sheer potency of the magic used to forge it. A sentient item behaves like a character, complete with personality quirks, ideals, bonds, and flaws. Most sentient items are weapons. Consumable items such as potions and scrolls are never sentient.

"The blade whispered to me as I drew it from the scabbard. Not in words — not at first. It was more like a current of warmth, a feeling of righteous anger that I knew was not my own. By the third day, it spoke my name. By the tenth, I wasn't sure whose thoughts were whose."
— Journal of Ser Valdris Thorn, Knight of the Radiant Order

Creating Sentient Items

Mental Ability Scores

A sentient item has Intelligence, Wisdom, and Charisma scores. Roll 4d6 drop the lowest for each, or assign scores appropriate to the item's nature. A battle-hungry greatsword might have high Charisma (force of personality) but low Wisdom (poor judgment). A contemplative spellbook might have towering Intelligence but modest Charisma.

Conflict & Control

A sentient item has a will of its own. If its wielder acts in a manner opposed to the item's alignment or purpose, conflict can arise. When conflict occurs:

Step 1: Contest. The item makes a Charisma check contested by the wielder's Charisma check.

Step 2: Demands. If the item wins, it may demand: to be carried at all times, that the wielder dispose of items the sentient item finds repugnant, that its goals be pursued exclusively, or that it be given to someone else.

Step 3: Consequences. If the wielder refuses to comply, the item can: suppress its activated properties, make attunement impossible, or attempt to take control of the wielder (the wielder is charmed by the item for 1d12 hours on a failed Charisma save, DC = 12 + item's Charisma modifier).

Roleplaying Sentient Item Conflicts

The best sentient item conflicts feel like a relationship, not a mechanic. The DM should voice the item, give it consistent personality traits, and let the tension build organically. A chaotic neutral blade that starts as a fun companion but gradually becomes possessive and demanding creates far more memorable gameplay than a simple contested Charisma check.

Chapter VI

Crafting & Forging

The creation of magic items is among the most closely guarded arts in the multiverse. It requires mastery of arcane theory, access to rare materials, and — for the most powerful items — sacrifices that few are willing to make. This chapter presents comprehensive rules for player characters and NPCs who wish to forge their own magical arms and armor.

The Crafting Process

Step 1: Formula. You must possess the item's formula — a document describing the item's creation process, required materials, and specific magical procedures. Formulas for common and uncommon items can sometimes be purchased (100–500 gp). Formulas for rare and higher items must be discovered through quests, research, or bargaining with powerful entities.

Step 2: Materials. You need a nonmagical version of the item (or the raw materials to create one), plus one or more special components. Higher-rarity items require rarer and more dangerous components — see the Materials section below.

Step 3: The Work. Each workweek of crafting requires 8 hours of work per day, 5 days per week. Multiple characters can combine their efforts, dividing the total time required among them. Each contributing character must meet the level and proficiency requirements.

Step 4: Complications. At the DM's discretion, crafting may trigger complications — rival interest, material degradation, magical mishaps, or the attention of extraplanar beings. Roll on the Crafting Complications table when appropriate.

Crafting Complications (d6)

1. A rival artificer learns of your work and attempts to steal the item or its formula.
2. The magical energies attract the attention of an extraplanar entity (demon, fey lord, or celestial).
3. A critical material component degrades during the process. You must acquire a replacement or accept a flaw in the final item.
4. The item develops an unintended minor magical property (roll on the Minor Magical Property table).
5. The enchantment process causes a wild magic surge. Roll on the Wild Magic Surge table.
6. No complication. The crafting proceeds without incident.

Chapter VIII

Design & Aesthetics

A magic item's visual design is as important as its mechanical properties. The way an item looks, feels, sounds, and even smells contributes to its presence in the narrative. This chapter provides DMs and players with tools and tables for designing visually distinctive and memorable magic items.

Visual Style by Rarity

As items increase in rarity, their visual signature becomes more dramatic. A common magic item might look nearly identical to its mundane counterpart with only a subtle tell — a faint shimmer, an unusual heft. A legendary item, on the other hand, should be unmistakable: a weapon that hums with visible power, armor that seems to drink in light, a staff crowned with a gem that pulses like a heartbeat.
