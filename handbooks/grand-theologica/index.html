Chapter I — Ao the Overgod

⚜
"I am above the gods, beyond the gods. I am that which holds the balance. I do not require worship, for I am not sustained by it. I simply am."— Attributed to Ao, during the Time of Troubles

Above all gods, beyond all pantheons, and utterly indifferent to mortal worship sits Ao — the Overgod of Realmspace. Ao is not a deity in any traditional sense. He does not answer prayers. He grants no spells. He holds no divine portfolio. No temple has ever been raised in his name that has received an answer, and no cleric has ever channeled his power, because Ao exists on a level of reality so far above the gods that even they are merely his stewards, tasked with maintaining the Balance of the cosmos.

The Nature of Ao

Ao's nature is fundamentally unknowable. He exists outside the Great Wheel cosmology entirely — he is not bound to any plane, any alignment, or any concept. The gods of Toril exist because Ao permits them to exist. Each deity holds a portfolio — a sphere of influence over some aspect of reality — and they hold that portfolio at Ao's sufferance. If a god fails in their duties, hoards power beyond their station, or allows their portfolio to fall into neglect, Ao can and will strip them of their divinity.

Ao answers to a being or force even higher than himself — a luminous presence he has been observed reporting to, though no god or mortal has ever identified this entity. Some Candlekeep scholars speculate this may be the DM — the narrative force of the multiverse itself — but such musings are considered heretical and absurd in equal measure.

Ao's primary concern is the Balance: the cosmic equilibrium between all forces — good and evil, law and chaos, life and death. The gods are the mechanisms by which this Balance is maintained. When the Tablets of Fate were stolen — the divine artifacts upon which every god's portfolio and duty were inscribed — Ao cast every single deity out of the heavens and forced them to walk Toril as mortals. This event, the Time of Troubles of 1358 DR, demonstrated Ao's absolute authority: gods died as mortals during this period, slain by adventurers and by each other.

The Tablets of Fate

The Tablets of Fate are a pair of stone artifacts upon which are inscribed the names, portfolios, and duties of every god in Realmspace. They are not merely a record — they are the mechanism of divine assignment itself. When the tablets were stolen by Bane and Myrkul (who sought to leverage them for greater power), the entire divine order of Faerûn was thrown into chaos. Ao's response was immediate and merciless: he stripped every god of their divine power and cast them into mortal forms upon Toril, declaring that none would return to the heavens until the tablets were recovered and returned.

The Tablets also serve as a covenant. They define the rules of engagement between deities — which portfolios belong to whom, the boundaries of divine influence, and the duties each god owes to the mortal world. When Ao rewrote the tablets after the Time of Troubles, he added a new edict: all gods must have worshippers, and their power would henceforth be directly tied to the faith of mortals. This fundamentally changed the divine economy of Faerûn, making worship not merely gratifying but existentially necessary.

Ao's Relationship with Mortals

Ao does not care about mortals. This is not cruelty — it is simply the nature of his station. Ao cares about the cosmic order, and mortals are part of that order, but he has no personal investment in their prayers, their struggles, or their fates. Mortals who attempt to worship Ao receive nothing. No divine magic flows from his name. A handful of cults have formed around Ao over the centuries, but they are universally powerless — their rituals hollow, their prayers unanswered, their faith unrewarded. This is by design. Ao's relationship is with the gods, not with their subjects.

Chapter II — The Greater Gods of Faerûn

⚜

The Greater Gods are the most powerful deities of the Faerûnian pantheon, holding the broadest and most critical portfolios. Their influence shapes the very fabric of reality.

Mystra NG
The Lady of Mysteries, the Mother of All Magic, Our Lady of Spells
Portfolio
Magic, spells, the Weave
Rank

Home Plane

Worshippers
Wizards, sorcerers, sages, artificers, anyone who uses the Art

Mystra is arguably the most important deity in the Forgotten Realms, for she is the keeper of the Weave — the fundamental fabric of magic that permeates all of Toril and allows both arcane and divine spellcasting to function. Without Mystra, the Weave unravels. When the Weave unravels, magic becomes wild, unpredictable, and catastrophic. This has happened twice in recorded history, and both times the consequences were apocalyptic.

The office of Mystra has been held by three distinct beings. The first was Mystryl, a being who existed since the creation of the Weave itself, who died absorbing the damage caused by the Netherese arcanist Karsus when he cast Karsus's Avatar — the only 12th-level spell ever successfully cast. Mystryl sacrificed herself to preserve the Weave, and was immediately reincarnated as the first Mystra, who rewrote the rules of magic, imposing a hard limit on spell levels (capping at 9th level) to prevent another Karsus event.

This first Mystra was slain during the Time of Troubles in 1358 DR by Helm, who was guarding the entrance to the Celestial Stairway under Ao's orders. After her death, the mortal wizard Midnight — who had been carrying a portion of Mystra's power — ascended to take the name Mystra, becoming the current incarnation of the goddess.

Mystra's church is one of the most widespread in Faerûn. Her clergy — known as the Servants of Mystery — believe that magic is a gift meant to be used, preserved, and spread. They oppose the hoarding of magical knowledge, the wanton destruction of magical items and sites, and the misuse of the Art. The most devout of her followers are the Chosen of Mystra — mortals imbued with a fragment of her divine power, including the famous Seven Sisters.

Selûne CG
Our Lady of Silver, the Moonmaiden, She Who Guides
Portfolio
The moon, stars, navigation, wanderers, questers, lycanthropes (good)

Worshippers

Selûne is one of the oldest deities in the Faerûnian pantheon, born from the primordial essence of Realmspace alongside her twin sister, Shar, before any other gods existed. The two sisters together created Chauntea — the embodiment of Toril itself — from the cosmic ether. But where Selûne poured light and warmth into creation, Shar sought to smother it in eternal darkness. Their conflict — the Eternal War — is one of the oldest and most fundamental struggles in the cosmology of the Forgotten Realms.

Selûne embodies light in darkness, hope in despair, and guidance for the lost. Her priestesses (the Silverstars) perform moonlit ceremonies, bless ships before voyages, and tend to those afflicted by unwanted lycanthropy. Good-aligned lycanthropes revere her as a protector.

Shar NE
Mistress of the Night, the Nightsinger, Lady of Loss, the Dark Lady
Portfolio
Darkness, caverns, dungeons, night, loss, forgetfulness, secrets, the Shadow Weave
Symbol

Domain

Home Plane
The Palace of Loss (within the Shadowfell)
Worshippers

Shar is the twin sister of Selûne and one of the most ancient beings in existence. She was born from the primordial darkness that existed before light — she is that darkness. While Selûne embraces creation and illumination, Shar embraces entropy, loss, and the eventual return of all things to the void.

Shar created the Shadow Weave — an alternative to Mystra's Weave that draws on the gaps and absences in the magical fabric. The Shadow Weave is darker, more subtle, and inherently corrupting. Her worshippers — the Sharrans — operate in secret cells called Dark Covens, infiltrating religions, governments, and organizations. Her church preys on the grieving, the heartbroken, and the desperate, offering the seductive comfort of forgetting. Her relationship with Shadowheart and the Dark Justiciars in Baldur's Gate 3 offered many modern players their first encounter with Shar's theology.

Tempus CN

Tempus is the god of war — all war, not merely righteous war or honorable war, but the full, ugly, beautiful, terrifying spectrum of armed conflict. He does not judge the cause for which one fights. He is chaotic neutral — he favors neither good nor evil in warfare. Nearly every soldier in Faerûn whispers a prayer to Tempus before battle. His church has only a few absolute commandments: never refuse a battle, never slay the wounded who yield, and never destroy a temple of any war god.

Kelemvor Lyonsbane is the current god of the dead — a position he inherited after the Time of Troubles. He is unique among death gods in that he is fundamentally fair. He does not revel in death. He is a judge, not a tyrant. In life, Kelemvor was a human mercenary cursed by his own bloodline — the Lyonsbane curse forced him to transform into a beast whenever he performed a selfless act. His ascension to godhood was a liberation.

Kelemvor's church is dedicated to three principles: comforting the dying, ensuring proper burial rites, and the absolute destruction of undead. His clergy — the Doomguides — are among the most feared opponents of necromancers in all Faerûn.

Tyr is the blind god of justice, and his blindness is not a weakness but a symbol: justice does not see station, wealth, or power. Tyr lost his right hand to the jaws of Kezef the Chaos Hound. He was part of the Triad — the alliance of Tyr, Ilmater, and Torm. Tyr was slain during the Spellplague era by Orcus but was restored during the Second Sundering.

Chauntea is one of the oldest gods — she is, in a sense, Toril itself given divine consciousness. She was created by Selûne and Shar together, before their great war began. She is the most widely worshipped deity among common folk, for while a peasant farmer may never encounter a wizard's spell, they interact with Chauntea's gift every day when they tend their fields.

Lathander is the god of the dawn — and by extension, of all beginnings, all new starts, all creative acts. There is a deep theological controversy: many scholars believe he is actually Amaunator — an ancient Netherese sun god — reborn. His temples face east, and dawn services are the most important rituals of his faith. The church despises undead as perversions of the natural cycle.

Helm is duty incarnate. During the Time of Troubles, Helm alone kept his divine power — because someone had to guard the Celestial Stairway. When Mystra attempted to return, Helm struck her down. He wept as he did it. He loved her. But his duty was absolute. This act defined his church for a generation. Helm was killed by Tyr in a misunderstanding but restored during the Second Sundering.

Silvanus is the supreme god of wild nature — not the tamed fields of Chauntea, but the untamed, primordial wilderness. His druids serve as wardens of the wild places. His church teaches the absolute sanctity of the natural balance: predators must hunt, prey must flee, forests must grow, fires must burn, and all things return to the earth.

Talos is raw, uncontrolled natural devastation given divine form, and he delights in it. He is worshipped primarily out of fear: sailors pray to Talos not out of devotion but out of desperate hope. His clergy — the Stormlords — revel in destruction, viewing themselves as agents of the natural entropy that Talos represents.

His church — the Dark Hand — operates as a military organization, establishing tyrannical regimes and corrupting governments.

Cyric is madness wearing a crown. He was a mortal thief who slew three gods during the Time of Troubles and ascended. He authored the Cyrinishad — a tome so potent that reading it (including Cyric himself) causes absolute conviction that Cyric is the most important being in the multiverse. Cyric read his own book and went permanently insane. His church is fractious and backstabbing by design.

Chapter III — The Intermediate Deities

Ilmater LG
The Crying God, the Broken God
Portfolio
Endurance, suffering, martyrdom, perseverance
Symbol
Bound hands, palms upward, with red cord
Home Plane
The Martyrdom (Bytopia)
Worshippers
The oppressed, monks, healers, those who suffer

Ilmater takes the suffering of others upon himself. His church runs hospitals, orphanages, and shelters. His monks practice endurance disciplines, believing that by suffering willingly, they reduce the suffering of the world. A member of the Triad alongside Tyr and Torm.

Torm LG
The True, the Loyal Fury
Portfolio
Duty, loyalty, obedience, paladins
Symbol
A white right gauntlet
Home Plane
Trueheart (Mount Celestia)
Worshippers
Paladins, knights, guardians, loyal soldiers

Torm was a mortal paladin who ascended through sheer devotion. He died during the Time of Troubles destroying Bane, and was resurrected by Ao for his sacrifice. His Order of the Golden Lion and Order of the Companions are legendary knightly orders.

Sune CG

Portfolio

Symbol
The face of a beautiful red-haired woman
Home Plane
Brightwater (Arborea)
Worshippers
Lovers, artists, half-elves, beauticians

Sune is the goddess of beauty and love in all their forms. Her Heartwarders serve as matchmakers, counselors, and artists. She despises ugliness of spirit more than ugliness of form, and her church teaches that cruelty and spite are the true disfigurements.

Mielikki NG

Worshippers
Rangers, druids, hunters, woodsmen, elves

Mielikki is Silvanus's most loyal subordinate and the direct patron of rangers across Faerûn. Her rangers patrol the borders between settled lands and wilderness, protecting both from each other.

Corellon's form is famously fluid. In the mythic past, Corellon's battle with Gruumsh cost the orc god his eye. Corellon's blood, spilled in that battle, is said to have given birth to the first elves.

Moradin LG

Moradin literally forged the first dwarves from iron and mithral on his cosmic anvil. His temples are always forges, and his clergy are always master craftsmen. Every perfectly forged blade is a prayer.

Lolth CE
The Spider Queen, Queen of the Demonweb Pits
Portfolio

Once an elven goddess named Araushnee, she betrayed the Seldarine and was cast into the Abyss, where she remade herself as the Spider Queen. She rules the drow through absolute cruelty: the strong dominate the weak, treachery is encouraged as natural selection.

Gruumsh CE

Gruumsh teaches that the orcs were cheated by the other creator gods, who divided the world among their races and left the orcs with nothing. What was not given must be taken by force.

Chapter XVII — Worship & Religious Practice

⚜

The Nature of Worship

Worship in Faerûn is not faith in the philosophical sense — no one doubts the gods exist. The question is "which god do I serve?" After Ao's edict, worship became existentially necessary for gods: more worshippers equals more power.

Polytheistic Practice

Most common folk are practical polytheists who pray to whichever god is relevant to their current need. A farmer prays to Chauntea for harvest, Talos to spare storms, Lathander for dawn, and Kelemvor when a loved one dies. Only the most devout dedicate themselves to a single god.

Holy Days

Major holy days are tied to the Harptos calendar. Midsummer is sacred to Sune and Lathander. The Feast of the Moon belongs to Kelemvor and Mystra. Highharvestide to Chauntea. Shieldmeet (every four years) is when divine magic is especially potent and great convocations are held.

Chapter XIX — The Great Wheel Cosmology

⚜
"Imagine a wheel. At the hub is the Material Plane. The Inner Planes form a ring of elemental fury. The Outer Planes encircle everything — a wheel of ideology made manifest, where belief shapes reality."— A primer from Sigil's Hall of Speakers

At the center sits the Material Plane. Surrounding it are the Transitive Planes — the Astral, the Ethereal, and the Shadowfell — as conduits between other planes. The Inner Planes form a ring of elemental energy: Fire, Water, Earth, Air, and the Positive and Negative Energy Planes. The Outer Planes form the outermost ring, arranged by alignment, where gods dwell and souls are judged after death.

The Outlands & Sigil

At the conceptual center of the Outer Planes lies the Outlands — a plane of true neutrality. At its center rises an infinitely tall spire, atop which floats Sigil, the City of Doors — ruled by the Lady of Pain, who bars deities from entering.

Chapter XXII — The Transitive Planes

⚜
The Astral Plane
The Silver Void

Silvery, weightless void connecting the Material Plane to the Outer Planes. You do not age here. The corpses of dead gods float here — continent-sized petrified bodies. The githyanki have built their empire here, using god-corpses as cities. Color pools serve as portals to the Outer Planes. The silver cord of astral projectors, if severed, means instant death.

The Ethereal Plane
The Misty Border

Ghostly border between Material and Inner Planes. The Border Ethereal overlaps the material world (ghosts exist here). The Deep Ethereal connects to Inner Planes and demiplanes — pocket dimensions like Ravenloft.

The Shadowfell
The Dark Mirror

Dark, twisted reflection of the Material Plane — dimmer, greyer, colder. Shar's Palace of Loss is here. Home to shadar-kai, shadow dragons, and sorrowsworn. The Domains of Dread (including Barovia) exist within it.

The Feywild
The Echo of Wonder

Bright mirror of the Shadowfell — oversaturated colors, stronger emotions, wilder nature. Time flows unpredictably. Ruled by archfey: Titania (Summer Court) and the Queen of Air and Darkness (Winter Court). Home to eladrin, satyrs, pixies, and dryads.

The Fugue Plane
Forgotten Realms Specific

Waystation of the dead. Every mortal soul goes here after death. Kelemvor judges them in the Crystal Spire. The faithful are claimed by their god's servants. The Faithless form the Wall of the Faithless.

Chapter XXIII — The Far Realm

⚜
The Far Realm
Beyond the Cosmology — The Outside

The Far Realm is not part of the Great Wheel. It exists outside the cosmology entirely. Physics, magic, time, space, identity, causality — none apply there. Looking at the Far Realm rewrites your mind. Its entities are the aberrations: mind flayers, aboleths, beholders. The Elder Evils — Hadar, Acamar, Gibbeth — dwell here. They are not gods in the Faerûnian sense. They are outside, and they want in.

The difference between the Far Realm and other planes cannot be overstated. The Abyss is evil but comprehensible. The Far Realm follows no rules. It is the antithesis of ordered reality, and its incursion into the multiverse is the one threat all gods — good, evil, lawful, chaotic — agree must be opposed.

Chapter XXIV — The Afterlife, the Faithless & the False

⚜

The Journey of a Soul

When a mortal dies, their soul departs for the Fugue Plane. Faithful worshippers are claimed by their deity's servants and escorted to the appropriate Outer Plane. Over centuries, petitioners are absorbed into the plane itself.

The Faithless

Those who worshipped no god are condemned to the Wall of the Faithless — a horrific barrier around the Crystal Spire made from writhing souls. They dissolve over millennia. The Wall is one of the most morally controversial elements of Realms cosmology.

The False

Those who betrayed their pledged god. Cast into the City of Judgment to be tormented. Many are traded to fiends who value souls as currency.

Soul Trade & Soul Coins

Mortal souls are commodities in the Lower Planes. Devils make bargains for souls, which become lemures in Hell. Soul coins — literal coins containing a trapped, suffering mortal — are the currency of Avernus.

Resurrection

Resurrection magic pulls a soul back from its afterlife. It requires the soul's willingness — a soul at peace can refuse. The process is traumatic. Kelemvor's church views it as acceptable but solemn.

Chapter XXV — The Blood War

⚜
"An infinity of demons against an infinity of devils, fighting forever. The most destructive, pointless conflict in existence — and it may be the only thing keeping the multiverse alive."— A'kin the Friendly Fiend
∞Duration
∞Casualties
3Fronts

Demons have infinite numbers but no discipline. Devils have discipline but finite numbers. Permanent stalemate. Key fronts: Avernus (Zariel's defense), the Grey Waste (neutral staging ground), and the River Styx (naval warfare on memory-erasing waters).

Zariel — a fallen solar angel who commands Hell's defense. Demogorgon — two-headed Prince of Demons. Orcus — Prince of Undeath who seeks to end it by ending all life.

The Yugoloth Conspiracy

Theory holds that yugoloths started the Blood War as a deliberate strategy — keeping both sides focused on each other to prevent either from achieving total dominion. The most successful con in the history of evil.

Why Good Gods Allow It

As long as evil fights itself, it's not united against good. If the Blood War ended, the winning faction would turn its full attention to the multiverse. The war is a containment strategy.

Chapter XXVII — Methods of Planar Travel

7th-level spell. Requires attuned tuning fork. Imprecise destination. Available to Clerics, Sorcerers, Warlocks, Druids.

9th-level spell. Precise destination anywhere. Opens a portal anyone can walk through. Can summon extraplanar creatures.

9th-level. Physical body stays behind, connected by silver cord. If cord is severed, you die.

No spell needed. Fairy rings → Feywild. Shadow pools → Shadowfell. Volcanic vents → Plane of Fire. Often well-known to locals.

Every doorframe in Sigil is potentially a portal. Each requires a specific "key" — a word, flower, emotion, or song.

Color Pools
Found in the Astral Plane. Gold = Celestia. Red = Nine Hells. Black = Abyss. Silver = Material Plane.

Yugoloths: Mezzoloth (soldiers) → nycaloth (enforcers) → arcanaloth (diplomats) → ultroloth (leaders). Mercenary fiends who sell to both sides of the Blood War.

Elementals & Genies

Djinn (Air, CG — friendliest). Efreeti (Fire, LE — slave-keeping tyrants). Marid (Water, CN — vain, capricious). Dao (Earth, NE — avaricious slavers). Mephits are small, mischievous elemental pests across all environments.

Aberrations

Mind Flayers: Psionic brain-eaters with tentacled faces. Reproduce via ceremorphosis (tadpole implantation). Led by Elder Brains.

Beholders: Floating paranoid spheres with antimagic central eye and ten magical eyestalks. Every beholder considers all others flawed abominations.

Aboleths: Ancient fish-beings who predate the gods. Perfect racial memory stretching to the dawn of time. They resent the divine order.

Undead

From mindless (skeletons, zombies) to sapient (ghosts, wraiths, vampires, liches). All draw from the Negative Energy Plane. All are abominations to Kelemvor — souls denied their rightful afterlife.
